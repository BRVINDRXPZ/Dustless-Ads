# Customer Language Bank

**Important:** these are search-intent and interview-language hypotheses, not verbatim customer quotes. They must not be attributed to real customers. Replace or validate them using permissioned reviews, call notes, Search Console, and owner interviews.

## Homeowner questions

| Moment | Language hypothesis | Content/response need |
|---|---|---|
| Problem | “Can these floors be saved?” | Restore/repair/replace decision framework |
| Dust | “How dusty will the house be?” | Exact containment and cleanup explanation; no zero-dust promise |
| Occupancy | “Do we have to move out?” | Variables, sequencing, cure/occupancy rules by verified system |
| Color | “Can you match the old floor?” | Honest matching limitations and sample process |
| Damage | “Can you replace only the damaged boards?” | Repair candidacy, sourcing, blend limits, minimum scope |
| Cost | “What changes the price?” | Scope drivers without invented ranges |
| Time | “How long before we can walk on it?” | Product/process-specific answer after verification |
| Historic | “Is this heart pine?” | Identification process; never guess from one photo |
| Finish | “Which sheen hides wear?” | Use-case consultation and care tradeoffs |
| Trust | “Who will be in my house?” | Verified team/process/insurance facts |
| Logistics | “What do we do with furniture?” | Preparation checklist and responsibility split |
| Smell/safety | “How strong is the smell?” | Verified product/SDS and ventilation guidance |
| Pets/kids | “When is it safe for pets and children?” | Manufacturer-specific cure/use instructions |
| Value | “Should I refinish or replace?” | Decision criteria based on condition and goals |

## Historic-property language

- “Keep the character.”
- “Save the original floors if possible.”
- “Repair around old patches.”
- “Match antique boards.”
- “Avoid making it look brand-new in the wrong way.”
- “Understand what can and cannot be sanded again.”

These are hypotheses reflecting common buyer concerns, not verified demand volume.

## Contractor/builder language

- “Can you price from plans and a scope?”
- “What do you need before mobilization?”
- “Can you provide COI and vendor paperwork?”
- “Who owns moisture/subfloor readiness?”
- “How quickly will change conditions be documented?”
- “Can you coordinate with painters, millwork, and punch?”
- “What is excluded?”
- “Will you communicate schedule risk early?”

## Commercial/hospitality language

- “Can the work be phased?”
- “What is the downtime?”
- “Can you work around operating hours?”
- “Which finish fits the traffic and maintenance plan?”
- “How will dust, odor, access, and adjacent spaces be controlled?”
- “Can you document safety and insurance requirements?”
- “Who coordinates reopening and cure restrictions?”

## Objection themes to validate

1. Fear of dust and disruption.
2. Uncertainty about whether restoration is possible.
3. Price comparison without comparable scope.
4. Concern about stain/color mismatch.
5. Timing around move-in, tenants, events, or reopening.
6. Trust, insurance, and crew accountability.
7. Confusion among screen-and-coat, sanding/refinishing, repair, and replacement.
8. Northshore travel/availability.

## Ethical language rules

- Say “dust-controlled” only after the process wording is verified; explain residual dust honestly.
- Say “historic” or name wood species only when documentation or competent identification supports it.
- Use real review language only with an approved source and permission/usage policy.
- Never convert these hypotheses into fake testimonials.
- Never guarantee color, invisibility of repairs, timing, cure, or outcome without a scope-specific basis.
